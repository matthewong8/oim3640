Final Paper Reflection
      Throughout this semester, I believe that my perception of what a negotiation should be or can be has changed. I began this course thinking that negotiation was mainly about haggling, saying the right things to get the best possible deal or maximize profit/loss because I wanted to get better in doing so. However, each exercise, simulation, and reading has revealed a much deeper depth to negotiation than simply haggling. I found myself challenged to grow both strategically, specifically how I plan to execute a specific negotiation according to a certain plan and emotionally, specifically how I choose to portray myself in various situations. This reflection recaps the most important lessons I learned about myself as the negotiator and illustrates how I plan to apply such lessons throughout my professional career. 
Strengths:
      One of my clearest strengths that emerged through this course is that I negotiate best when I prepare deeply. Early exercises like Bakra Beverage taught me a great deal about extensively preparing for a negotiation because Bakra Beverage was our first simulation that we utilized the 7 Elements Worksheet in order to know the ins and outs of the case. In prior negotiations, I had good senses of my goals that I want to achieve but never really had a structured plan to gather all the details/data of each case and each helpful element that can form a certain strategy based on various situations. Previously, Iï¿½ve always dealt with struggles in relation being anchored in ways that would require me to base my next offer according to the anchor which many times, places me in a tough position. Nevertheless, I realized afterward that knowing my BATNA, forming a specific anchor myself, and identifying both partiesï¿½ interests not just positions allowed me to be far more effective during the Bakra simulation. Going into that negotiation, I spent a significant amount of time working through the 7 Elements Worksheet, mapping out BATNAs, clarifying interests, developing legitimacy standards, brainstorming options, and outlining possible commitments. Because I had already identified my priorities such as long-term relationship, performance incentives, exclusivity requirements, and contingency offers based on market penetration, I entered the discussion with a lot of clarity and purpose, using important criteria to justify my offers which was very important in Bakra Beverage. Without understanding the 7 elements clearly, I wouldnï¿½t be able to gain as much value as I did while negotiating with BebsiCo. Walking into a negotiation already understanding all 7 elements and creative options gave me confidence and strategic direction which I believe directly formed the deal I achieved.
      Another major realization was that my greatest strength as a negotiator is my instinctive nature to commit to an interest-based approach. Throughout this course, I found that I constantly gravitate towards trying to create value in every negotiation instead of prioritizing a win/lose situation. I preferred uncovering the reason behind the other partyï¿½s positions and trying to build solutions that meet the needs of both sides. This was definitely prominent in Camilia Pictures where instead of reacting defensively to the emotional tension between the parties, I focused on clarifying Reynoldsï¿½ underlying interests which were creative control, reputation, and ensuring the documentary would not be shelved, while also addressing Statlerï¿½s core concerns around budget discipline, long-term partnership, and keeping Reynolds in the business. Framing the negotiation in a way that emphasizes my interest to find a common ground especially with Reynoldsï¿½ concerns allowed me to generate options like co-distribution and performance-based conditions that addressed the root issues rather than getting stuck haggling about each otherï¿½s positions. This strength was also applied in Harborco, where with six different parties and highly conflicting demands, a positional mindset would have destroyed the negotiation immediately. Instead, I used an interest-based approach to navigate the complexity, identifying what each stakeholder actually cared about (DCRï¿½s need for balanced investment, the Governorï¿½s political stance, the Unionï¿½s job access concerns, the Environmental Leagueï¿½s ecological safeguards, and the Portï¿½s fear of competitive disadvantage). As Harborco, I tried to balance every possible issue to meet the needs of everyone which made it much easier to craft different combinations. Although taking a more power-based approach couldï¿½ve been more profitable to Harborco considering I had veto power and the actual outcome came out to be no agreement, I believe that I was able to relate with most of the different stakeholders and there was not one moment of hostility. These experiences revealed that this approach is not a total weakness but a strategic advantage to create room for more value captured agreements.
      Along with creating value, I believe that my ability to logroll has been one of my biggest strengths to create value. Identifying where opposing parties prioritize issues differently and using those differences to build agreements that benefit everyone has been a very effective method this semester. Across multiple simulations, I realized that I naturally look for mismatched priorities not as obstacles but as opportunities to maximize value. In Harborco for example, each stakeholder group cared about a different aspect of the project, where the Union prioritized job access, the Environmental League prioritized ecological safeguards over industry mix, the Governor prioritized federal investment over environmental upgrades, and other Ports cared about compensation. Noticing these differences helped me form proposals, where giving one stakeholder something they valued highly cost Harborco relatively little. For example, the Environmental League was open to a clean/dirty mix as long as we maintain and repair the ecosystem and I was willing to provide more compensation to other ports because I convinced the Union to take a 1:1 quota. Moreover, in Mexico Venture, we valued maintaining family control, ensuring long-term business sustainability, and protecting the role of our brother. Inherently, this differed vastly from their focus on revenue-based commissions and rapid sales scaling. This difference gave me room to create value by shifting the conversation toward profit-linked incentives that aligned with both our goals to keep the business growing. For example, instead of hiring more sales, we would instead make trainers and our brother help the sales team during their free time while still prioritizing their main role. Simultaneously, we will use the money that could be used to hire more sales employees to invest more in marketing which will be a more effective method to gain sales. These options allowed both parties to still achieve the goal of better performance while allowing us to preserve our teamwork mentality and reduce financial volatility. Thus, these exercises showed me that logrolling is one of my clearest negotiation strengths where I can juggle multiple issues at once and identify where parties value issues differently to capture the most value that are more efficient and collaborative. 
Challenges:

This was seen in Camilia Pictures, where I really toned down my stance and the framing of my argument because I wanted to prevent emotions from escalating since we desperately wanted Reynolds to remain in the company. Even when mentioning strong data/criteria to support my claims, I tend to soften the blow to keep the rapport which at times may weaken my position. In Chuckï¿½s Wagon, because my reservation value and my counterpartï¿½s reservation value was too different and there didnï¿½t seem to be a ZOPA that made much sense in terms of making my client happy, I found myself over conceding too much on the value of the properties and its amenities. These situations made me realize that my desire to maintain good relationships, while a strength in many ways, can also hold me back when I need to argue more assertively to protect my own interests.
      Additionally, a frequent challenge that I face as a negotiator is the tendency to get anchored by the other sideï¿½s opening value. In multiple negotiations, I found myself giving up too easily on my counter anchors. A good example of this was seen through the Job Offer negotiations. Nevertheless, this situation made me realize that I need to be more aware of getting anchored by others because it is a powerful tool to easily maximize individual value of what they want if framed well. While I am often collaborative, I often allow anchors to influence my expectations which needs to be changed.
      In relation to getting anchored, I think a prominent challenge I also realize through this course is letting relationship and coalition building outweigh goals that align with my interests and what seems like the most optimal outcome for myself. In certain situations, this can be very helpful. For example, in Camilia pictures, I hesitated to assert my needs because I didnï¿½t want to disrupt the friendly tone of the conversation which in theory is the right decision because Reynolds could easily walk away from the table if things go wrong considering there was heavy tension between my client and Reynolds. In all these negotiations, my instinct to preserve relationships led myself to underclaim my own personal goals. This challenge doesnï¿½t mean my relationship approach is a weakness, it is a strength, but I do need to maintain the ï¿½strictly businessï¿½ mindset and hold my ground, even when situations are uncomfortable.

Guidelines for Future Negotiations:
Guideline 1: Always prepare using the full 7 Elements before every negotiation even when the negotiation seems simple
Why this is Important:
- This course made it clear that in depth preparation for every negotiation definitely makes me much more confident and purposeful in the things I say. According to Wheeler, preparation reduces performance anxiety that many negotiators experience before going into a negotiation because it helps understand your fears (Wheeler, Overcoming Stage Fright - How to Prepare, P. 1). Bakra Beverage was the most vivid example because completing the 7 Elements Worksheet forced me to understand my BATNA, interests, criteria, options, and possible commitments (Patton, Negotiation Power). This structure helped me prevent being heavily anchored because it justifies my offers with objective criteria and gave me the confidence to push for my interests more clearly. This will definitely help me overcome the issue of being anchored heavily by who I am negotiating with because this will give me a great look into my own and my counterpartï¿½s interests, BATNA, and options. 
Risk and Limitations:
- Over preparation could lead to being stiff and unflexible. If I become too attached to my planned script, I might miss creative solutions that emerge in the negotiation. This guideline will only work if I ensure that my plan is objective enough because we know that a negotiation can be very ambiguous and if prepared towards a certain strategy, this may be more of a disadvantage. We learned in class that we should react differently under certain circumstances and so following the plan fully can be very limiting if the negotiation does not pan out the way we think. Additionally, prioritizing the elements that matter most is very important because the 7 elements worksheet should be used to understand your goals and strategy better instead of a cheat sheet to copy from.
Guideline 2: Shift the conversation back to interests and do not focus on positions if the negotiation feels emotional or you are simply stuck at an issue.
Why this is Important:
- My biggest strength is my interest-based approach and when I use this approach, I tend to create value and resolve disputes productively. Using this approach is helpful to create an atmosphere for collaboration between both parties (Lytle, Brett, & Shapiro, The Strategic Use of Interest Rights Power, P. 12). Remember Camilia Pictures, transforming a tense dispute into an interactive discussion. This will also prevent my challenge of getting anchored or reacting defensively because it allows parties to leverage common interests instead of defending positions (Fisher & Ury, Getting to YES, Ch. 3). Getting to know your counterpartï¿½s interests are immensely beneficial to logroll when stuck or find multiple issues to solve by creating creative options to focus on creating overall value. A negotiation where both parties win is still a win for you.
Risk and Limitations:
- Not every situation calls for an interest-based approach. At times, when you have clear basis/data/criteria over a certain situation that backs up your argument/claim, you need to know when to apply a rights-based or power-based approach as well especially in disputes (Lytle, Brett, & Shapiro, The Strategic Use of Interest Rights Power, P. 1-2). Wheeler explains the cruciality of the first 5 minutes which can entirely affect how the rest of the negotiation goes (Wheeler, Crucial First Five Minutes, P. 1). Moreover, not every counterpart is willing to discuss interests openly and therefore, you need to learn how to ask meaningful questions to get to know their interests. Another option is to simply shoot out a proposal and see how your counterpart reacts to that which is usually a signal of where their interest lies (Wheeler, Crucial First Five Minutes, P. 4). It is important to also clarify that some people may take advantage of your transparency as well if they view this as a sign of weakness. To prevent this, make sure that your main priority is to find out what your counterpartï¿½s interests are and do not simply reveal your own. 
Guideline 3: Go to the balcony and think about whether or not I am simply trying to preserve the relationship or avoid tension before making concessions.
Why this is Important:
- Throughout this semester, I believe that consciously examining my motivations and purpose before making final decisions in concessions are very crucial. In negotiating, you have to be very conscious with what you agree to because your counterpart will take your word for it. Once you agree to something or say something, it is not simple to retract your statement because in my eyes, it is a sign of lack of confidence, trust, and respect. Thus, going to the balcony and thinking for a while is a very wise decision to make when you are placed in a tough spot because it allows you to think clearly and for your benefit (Ury, Getting Past No, Ch. 1). Do not try and force an answer because you could get trapped by your own consequences. The three most common ways of reacting are giving in, striking back, and breaking off, all which can be prevented if you simply take the time to think and recoup your thoughts (Ury, Getting Past No, Ch. 1). This guideline is specifically mentioned as a self-check to myself during complex situations.
Risks and Limitations:
- Conflicting motivations and emotional traps, where the desire to be liked, to appear reasonable or even to avoid conflict actually pulls people away from their actual interests (Malhotra, Negotiation Genius, Ch. 5). Emotional traps are great risks because often times, negotiators feel the need to be liked and seen as fair by your counterpart, and it can lead you to do things you may regret in the future. Moreover, often times I over concede due to feeling bad or feeling unreasonable to ask for more which is a matter of framing. The way in which your counterpart frames things may change the way we feel and could create conflicting motivations within oneself which could be to their advantage due to careless actions that can be done on our end (Malhotra, Negotiation Genius, Ch. 4). Thus, to overcome the susceptibility of framing, take a moment to go through options or come into the negotiation with a pre-planned exit strategy (Malhotra, Negotiation Genius, Ch. 4). A limitation of doing this is overdoing the act of thinking consciously. Do not use this guideline as a way to become more aggressive or fully change your tone to get your way. This should be a way to think more clearly of your next step and not retaliate against your counterpart.
Guideline 4: Search for differences in priorities (logrolling) and form creative options instead of easily conceding.
Why it is important:
- As seen throughout this semester, logrolling has been a very valuable strategy to come to a solution when there are multiple issues being discussed at the table. Logrolling is one of the best opportunities to create value because it allows you to ï¿½trade across issuesï¿½ (Malhotra, Negotiation Genius, Ch. 2). For me, this guideline plays directly into one of my strongest abilities which is seeing the negotiation as a group of interacting issues rather than a single win/lose outcome. A logrolling mindset helps me avoid the trap of conceding on high value issues, but instead enables me to maintain a collaborative tone while still achieving my core interests (Malhotra, Negotiation Genius, Ch. 2).
Risks and Limitations:
- The limitation of logrolling is the fact that it requires a lot of detailed information and transparency. Because logrolling creates value through trading on different priorities, negotiators need to know what these priorities are not only for themselves but for the other party as well. Thus, for logrolling to succeed, transparency must be present in the negotiation to maximize the value created. If not, it will be hard to know exactly what different issues can be traded with. If the other party seems stiff, look into asking questions or proposing a deal to get to know their interests more. Logrolling can also be seen as a sign of trust because it shows that both parties are willing to work with one another to find a common ground.
